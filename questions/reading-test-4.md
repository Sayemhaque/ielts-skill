### The Domestication of the Horse

The domestication of the horse changed the course of human history. This animal went from being a source of meat to a key tool for travel, war, and farming. Before they were domesticated, wild horses lived in large groups across the Eurasian steppes. This area stretches from Eastern Europe all the way to Central Asia and contains vast grasslands. We know from bones found at ancient campsites that early humans hunted horses for food. Some of these sites contain very large numbers of horse remains. This tells us that horses were a main source of food for many early groups. The move from hunting to keeping and then riding horses took place over thousands of years. It had a very deep effect on how human societies developed.

For a long time, most experts agreed that the first horse domestication took place on the Eurasian steppes. They believed it happened in the area of the Botai culture, in what is now northern Kazakhstan, around 3500 BCE. Early digs at Botai sites found traces of mare's milk on clay pots. This suggests that the people there not only kept horses but also used them for milk products. In addition, the teeth of horse skulls found at these sites showed clear signs of bit wear. This is strong evidence that the Botai put bits in the mouths of their horses. It means the animals were being ridden or used to pull loads. The combination of dairy use and riding equipment found together at one site was very unusual for that time period. Together, the milk remains on pots and the bit wear on teeth were seen as the oldest clear proof of horse domestication. For several decades, most archaeologists accepted the Botai culture as the clear starting point for horse domestication.

However, a major genetic study published in 2018 made the story much more complex and surprising. A team of international researchers read the DNA of ancient horse remains from all over Eurasia. To the surprise of the science world, the DNA tests showed that the Botai horses were not the ancestors of today's domestic horses. Instead, the Botai horses were the ancestors of Przewalski's horse. This is a short, brown animal that experts had always believed to be the last truly wild horse species. But the new findings meant that Przewalski's horse is actually feral. This means it came from horses that were once domesticated by the Botai but later escaped or were set free into the wild. As a result, scientists had to restart the search for the true ancestors of modern domestic horses.

The genetic evidence now points to a different area as the birthplace of the modern horse. This is the western Eurasian steppes, more specifically the Volga-Don region of present-day Russia. The domestication there took place around 2200 BCE. Horses from this area had a genetic change related to a gene called DMRT3. This gene affects how a horse moves its legs and walks. The change allows for ambling, which is a smooth way of walking with four beats. This would have made these horses much more comfortable to ride over long distances. This genetic feature spread at the same time as human populations were growing quickly across the steppes. This suggests that these special horses gave their riders a major advantage when moving from place to place and when trading goods with other groups.

The impact of the domestic horse on human societies was very large and wide-ranging. With the development of the chariot and later, riding itself, the horse changed the nature of war. It helped create mounted nomad empires such as the Scythians, who were famous for their skills on horseback. The horse also made long-distance communication and trade possible in ways that had never been seen before. It brought distant cultures closer together across the huge spaces of the Eurasian steppes. The horse became a sign of wealth and high social position. It was deeply woven into the stories, myths, and daily lives of the civilisations it helped to build. Without the domestication of this one animal, the history and makeup of the ancient world would have been totally and permanently different.

### Vertical Farming: Agriculture Reaches for the Sky

**A** By the middle of this century, the global population is projected to exceed nine billion, with nearly 70 percent of people living in cities. Feeding these populations using traditional farming methods presents a major challenge. Good farmland is shrinking because the soil is wearing out and cities are spreading into the countryside. At the same time, the environmental cost of carrying food over very long distances is becoming harder to justify. In response, a radical alternative has gained support among city planners and farming experts: vertical farming. This idea involves growing crops in stacked layers inside controlled buildings. These are often set up in old warehouses, shipping containers, or specially built skyscrapers. The plants are grown without soil, using methods such as hydroponics, where roots sit in nutrient-rich water, or aeroponics, where they are sprayed with a mist of nutrients. The aim is to produce large amounts of food in a small space that is close to where people actually live, cutting down on the need for long-distance transport.

**B** The best-known supporter of vertical farming is Dr. Dickson Despommier, a microbiologist who developed the concept in the early 2000s. Despommier believes that vertical farming could change food production by greatly reducing the amount of land needed for agriculture. He estimates that a single 30-story vertical farm could feed up to 50,000 people while taking up no more space than a single city block. In addition, by controlling temperature, humidity, and artificial light, these facilities could grow crops all year round, with no risk from droughts, frosts, or changing seasons. This controlled setting also removes the need for chemical pesticides, since the closed spaces keep out most insects and other pests.

**C** The technology behind vertical farming is quite advanced. Instead of soil, plants are grown in an artificial material and fed with nutrient solutions (hydroponics) or sprayed with nutrients in the form of a fine mist (aeroponics). Light comes from very efficient LED lamps that are set to the exact colours that plants need for growth, mainly red and blue. Dr. Toyoki Kozai, a leading researcher in plant factories, has shown that adjusting these LED colours can increase plant growth by as much as 50 percent compared to growing outdoors, while using far less water than normal irrigation. Kozai's studies focus on what he calls "light-use efficiency" — working out the exact number of light particles needed so that as little energy as possible is wasted during the growing process.

**D** However, vertical farming faces serious money and energy problems. Dr. Louise Manning, a food policy researcher, notes that the start-up costs for a commercial vertical farm are extremely high. The price of advanced climate control equipment, LED lighting systems, and automated harvesting machines means that only expensive, fast-growing crops — such as lettuce, herbs, and small green vegetables such as microgreens — can make a profit at the moment. Basic food crops like wheat and rice, which need a lot of space and energy to produce useful amounts, are still not worth growing in vertical systems. Manning also points to a key problem: although vertical farming reduces the distance food has to travel, the huge amount of electricity needed for lighting and climate control often leads to a larger carbon footprint than traditional farming, unless the power comes mainly from renewable sources such as solar or wind energy.

**E** The social effects of vertical farming are also being questioned. Dr. Stan Cox, an agricultural scientist, argues that vertical farming could create a two-level food system. He believes that the high cost of producing food in these facilities will result in expensive vegetables that only wealthy people can buy, doing little to help poorer communities who struggle to get fresh food. Cox says the answer to urban food shortages lies in community-based gardens at ground level and wider changes to how food is distributed, rather than expensive technological fixes. He also warns that separating food production from the natural environment could make city people even more disconnected from understanding where their food actually comes from and how it is grown.

**F** Despite these concerns, the vertical farming business is growing fast, helped by improvements in renewable energy and falling costs for LED technology. Supporters argue that as solar and wind power become cheaper and more effective, the environmental and financial costs of running these facilities will drop sharply. In the meantime, mixed approaches are starting to appear. Some city planners are including smaller vertical farms in public housing projects, aiming to provide fresh local food to poorer communities while covering some running costs through government subsidies. Whether vertical farming becomes a central part of urban food supply or stays a minor supplier of high-priced greens depends heavily on how quickly these energy and cost problems can be solved.

### The Fermi Paradox: The Great Silence

In the summer of 1950, the Nobel Prize-winning physicist Enrico Fermi was having a casual lunch with colleagues at the Los Alamos laboratory when the conversation turned to the possibility of extraterrestrial life. Given the immense size and age of the universe, the existence of millions of Earth-like planets, and the likelihood that many of them are billions of years older than our own, the probability of advanced alien civilisations seemed high. Yet, if such civilisations exist, where are they? Why have we found no credible evidence of their existence? This simple yet profound question has become known as the Fermi Paradox—the glaring contradiction between the high probability of alien life and the lack of observational evidence for it.

To quantify the probability of intelligent life beyond Earth, astronomers often turn to the Drake Equation, formulated by astrophysicist Frank Drake in 1961. The equation breaks the question down into a series of variables, including the rate of star formation, the fraction of stars with planets, and the probability of life evolving on those planets. While the equation does not provide a definitive answer, it underscores a crucial point: even if the probability of life evolving on any given planet is vanishingly small, the sheer number of stars in the galaxy means that thousands of intelligent civilisations should exist. The paradox, therefore, demands an explanation for the "Great Silence"—the absence of any detectable signals or physical evidence of these civilisations. This silence has persisted despite decades of dedicated searching using increasingly sensitive equipment.

One class of solutions to the paradox suggests that the Great Silence exists because intelligent life is exceedingly rare. This concept is encapsulated in the "Great Filter" hypothesis, which posits that there is a highly improbable step in the evolutionary timeline of any civilisation that prevents it from advancing to the stage of interstellar communication. The critical question is whether this filter lies behind us or ahead of us. If the filter is in our past—such as the transition from single-celled to multicellular life, or the leap to human-level intelligence—then humanity is a remarkable exception, having overcome a hurdle that almost no other species has managed. In this scenario, the Great Silence is the natural consequence of a universe where intelligent life is a statistical anomaly, which would make our existence a rare and precious accident.

Conversely, if the Great Filter lies ahead of us, the implications are deeply unsettling. This would mean that while many civilisations have reached or surpassed our level of technological advancement, they have all been destroyed before they could colonise the galaxy. The filter could be a natural phenomenon, such as a catastrophic gamma-ray burst, or it could be self-inflicted, through nuclear war, climate collapse, or the creation of hostile artificial intelligence. This interpretation suggests that humanity's future is bleak and that our technological progress may contain the seeds of our inevitable destruction.

An alternative class of solutions argues that advanced civilisations do exist but remain undetected for other reasons. The "Zoo Hypothesis" proposes that extraterrestrial civilisations are aware of our presence but deliberately avoid contact, observing us like animals in a zoo to allow us to evolve naturally without interference. A more technologically grounded explanation is the "Dark Forest" theory, popularised by the science fiction author Liu Cixin. This theory posits that the universe is a dark forest, and every civilisation is a silent hunter. In a cosmos where resources are finite and the intentions of others are unknown, the safest strategy for survival is to remain hidden; any civilisation that reveals its location will inevitably be destroyed by a preemptive strike from a fearful neighbour. Under this grim logic, the Great Silence is not a sign of emptiness, but a symptom of universal terror and mutual distrust among civilisations.

Finally, there is the possibility that we are simply listening in the wrong way. Human efforts to detect extraterrestrial intelligence, such as the Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence (SETI), have primarily focused on radio signals. However, a civilisation thousands of years more advanced than our own may have abandoned radio communication in favour of more sophisticated methods, such as modulated neutrino beams or gravitational waves, which we currently lack the technology to detect. Alternatively, they may have transitioned into a virtual reality, existing within supercomputers and having no interest in the physical universe outside. A further possibility is that intelligent life is so different from us that we would not recognise its signals even if they passed right through our detection systems. In this view, the Great Silence is a failure of imagination on our part, an assumption that alien minds would communicate in ways we currently comprehend.

The resolution of the Fermi Paradox remains elusive and the scientific debate continues today among many astronomers and physicists. Whether we are uniquely alone, precariously poised on the brink of destruction, hiding in a hostile cosmos, or merely deaf to the chatter of more advanced beings, the answer will profoundly alter our understanding of our place in the universe. As the search continues, the silence itself serves as a mirror, forcing us to confront uncomfortable questions about our own survival and the limits of human perception. For now, the question Fermi asked over seventy years ago still has no agreed answer.
