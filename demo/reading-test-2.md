### The Development of the Vacuum Flask

Containers that keep drinks hot or cold are now common household objects, but their original purpose was scientific rather than domestic. The vacuum flask was developed in the late nineteenth century by the Scottish physicist James Dewar, who needed a vessel that could store extremely cold gases during laboratory experiments. In 1892, Dewar created a double-walled glass container with most of the air removed from the space between the walls. This empty space, or vacuum, greatly reduced the transfer of heat. The invention was first used to hold liquefied gases such as oxygen and nitrogen, which boil at very low temperatures.

Before Dewar's work, scientists studying low-temperature materials often relied on ordinary glass vessels packed in ice or salt mixtures. These containers were useful for short demonstrations, but they could not preserve liquefied gases for long periods. Evaporation was rapid, and measurements became difficult because the contents warmed quickly. Dewar's flask allowed researchers to observe cold substances more steadily and to transport small quantities between parts of a laboratory. In this sense, the container was not an isolated curiosity but part of a wider effort to make experimental physics more precise.

The principle behind the flask is based on limiting three forms of heat transfer: conduction, convection, and radiation. Conduction occurs when heat moves through solid material, such as metal or glass. Convection happens when heat is carried by moving air or liquid. Radiation transfers heat through waves, even across empty space. Dewar's design reduced conduction and convection by creating a vacuum between two layers of glass. Because there was almost no air in this gap, heat could not easily pass from one wall to the other. To reduce radiation, the inner surfaces were coated with a thin reflective layer, usually silver, which sent heat back toward its source.

Early vacuum flasks were fragile because they were made entirely of glass. In laboratories, they were usually held inside protective metal cases or wooden boxes to prevent breakage. Dewar did not patent his invention, partly because he regarded it as a piece of scientific equipment. Around 1904, two German glassblowers, Reinhold Burger and Albert Aschenbrenner, recognised that the same technology could be used to keep soup, coffee, and other liquids warm outside the laboratory. Burger patented a version with a stronger outer casing and later helped market it under the name Thermos, which was chosen after a public competition. The word came from the Greek word for heat.

The early commercial versions were promoted to doctors, travellers, and people who worked outdoors. Advertisements emphasised practical situations: hot tea on a winter journey, cold milk for an infant, or soup carried to a workplace. The flask was also useful in warm climates because it could slow the melting of ice and keep drinking water cool. These examples helped the public understand a scientific idea through everyday needs.

As production expanded, manufacturers improved both the strength and convenience of the flask. Cork stoppers were gradually replaced by screw caps, which reduced leaks and allowed the cap to serve as a drinking cup. By the 1920s, vacuum flasks were being used by factory workers, railway passengers, hikers, and schoolchildren. A typical domestic flask could keep liquid hot for several hours, while larger insulated containers were used by hospitals and catering companies. During the same period, some companies introduced wide-mouthed food flasks that could carry stews or rice dishes rather than drinks.

Modern vacuum flasks usually use stainless steel instead of glass. Steel is less likely to shatter and can withstand repeated outdoor use. Many current models have copper or aluminium reflective layers, pressure-fitted lids, and silicone seals. Although the materials have changed, the basic physics remains close to Dewar's original design: two walls, a vacuum between them, and a reflective surface to slow heat movement. The same principle is also used in shipping containers for vaccines, insulated boxes for laboratory samples, and large tanks that store liquid nitrogen. Today, a simple flask may be sold as a travel accessory, but it still depends on a design created for experiments with some of the coldest substances known.

The popularity of reusable bottles has given the vacuum flask a new role in efforts to reduce disposable packaging. Cafes, schools, and offices increasingly encourage people to carry their own insulated containers rather than buy single-use cups or plastic bottles. While this environmental use was not part of Dewar's original purpose, it shows how a laboratory invention can continue to change as social habits and materials technology develop.

### Greening the Roofs of Cities

**A** As urban populations increase, city governments are searching for ways to make dense neighbourhoods cooler, cleaner, and more pleasant without using large areas of land. One proposal that has gained attention is the installation of green roofs, in which vegetation is planted on top of buildings above a waterproof layer. Supporters argue that rooftops are an underused resource in crowded cities, while critics question whether the benefits justify the cost. The debate has become more urgent as many cities face hotter summers, heavier rainfall, and rising demand for public green space.

Although rooftop planting is sometimes presented as a recent innovation, the basic idea is older than many assume. Turf roofs were used on traditional buildings in parts of northern Europe because they offered insulation and made use of local materials. The contemporary version is different: it combines lightweight growing media, drainage mats, root barriers, and selected plant species that can survive wind and shallow soil. This technical development has allowed planners to consider green roofs for offices, apartment blocks, schools, and transport buildings.

**B** Environmental planners often point first to temperature reduction. Conventional roofs made of concrete, asphalt, or dark tiles absorb heat during the day and release it slowly at night. This contributes to the urban heat island effect, in which built-up districts remain warmer than surrounding rural areas. According to Dr Priya Menon, an environmental engineer at Delhi Metropolitan University, a planted roof can reduce surface temperatures by 20 to 35 degrees Celsius on a summer afternoon compared with a bare roof. She argues that even when the effect inside a single building is modest, a network of green roofs across a district can lower local air temperatures and reduce pressure on air-conditioning systems.

**C** A second claimed benefit concerns stormwater management. In many cities, heavy rain quickly runs from hard surfaces into drains, increasing the risk of local flooding. The Urban Water Alliance, a consultancy that advises local authorities, reports that extensive green roofs can retain between 40 and 60 per cent of annual rainfall, depending on soil depth and plant type. Its 2023 survey of municipal projects found that the greatest benefit occurred during ordinary rainfall rather than during exceptional storms. For this reason, the organisation recommends green roofs as one part of a wider drainage strategy rather than as a complete replacement for pipes, tanks, and river defences.

**D** The economic case is less straightforward. Developers often resist green roofs because they add structural weight and require specialist installation. The Property Owners Federation argues that retrofitting older buildings can be expensive, especially when roofs must be strengthened before planting. Maintenance is another concern: plants may need irrigation during dry periods, and drainage outlets must be checked to prevent blockages. However, architect Luis Ortega of the Barcelona Design Institute suggests that these costs are often exaggerated. He notes that a well-designed roof can protect waterproof membranes from direct sunlight, extending their life by more than a decade in some commercial buildings.

Insurance and regulation add further complexity. Some building managers worry that a planted roof could hide leaks until damage has spread, while fire regulations may restrict the use of dry grasses in certain climates. In response, suppliers have developed modular trays that can be lifted for inspection and plant mixes that retain moisture without becoming waterlogged.

**E** Social benefits are discussed, although they vary with the design. Some roofs are simple ecological layers, inaccessible to residents, while others are built as gardens with paths, seating, and views. Studies of apartment blocks in Singapore indicate that shared rooftop gardens may encourage informal contact among neighbours who rarely meet at street level. Yet community access brings additional safety requirements, including guardrails, lighting, and supervision. Dr Hannah Clarke, a public health researcher in Melbourne, cautions that rooftop gardens should not be treated as substitutes for parks at ground level, particularly for children and elderly residents who may find roof access inconvenient.

**F** Most specialists now view green roofs as useful but limited tools. They can cool roof surfaces, slow rainwater runoff, and create small habitats for insects and birds, but their value depends heavily on climate, building type, and maintenance budgets. Dr Menon argues for targeted subsidies in districts with severe heat problems, while the Urban Water Alliance favours incentives near overloaded drainage networks. Ortega believes that planners should include green roofs in new developments from the beginning rather than adding them later. The emerging consensus is therefore not that every roof should become a garden, but that carefully chosen roofs can contribute to healthier and more resilient cities.

This selective approach also affects how success is measured. A roof designed mainly for drainage should be judged by the water it retains, not by the number of visitors it attracts. A roof intended to improve biodiversity may need native flowering plants rather than lawns and benches. As a result, cities that adopt green-roof policies increasingly require clear objectives before public money is offered.

### The Productive Role of Forgetting

Forgetting is commonly treated as a defect in human cognition: names disappear at awkward moments, appointments are missed, and facts once learned for examinations seem to fade almost deliberately. Yet this negative view is incomplete. A mind that retained every detail with equal force would not necessarily be wiser or more efficient. The central argument of this passage is that forgetting is not merely a failure of memory but a selective process that helps people generalise, adapt, and make decisions in changing environments. The problem, then, is not why memory sometimes loses information, but how the mind determines which information is worth preserving.

The assumption that more memory is always better has been encouraged by metaphors that compare the mind to a library, a camera, or a computer drive. Such comparisons are useful in limited ways, but they can be misleading because human memory is reconstructed whenever it is used. Remembering involves selecting, interpreting, and sometimes reshaping fragments of earlier experience. If forgetting is examined within this active system, it begins to look less like a hole in storage and more like one of the conditions that allows storage to remain usable.

One influential account comes from research on interference. According to this view, memories compete with one another when they share similar cues. A person who parks in a different place every morning may struggle to remember today's location because yesterday's and Monday's locations are also linked to the cue "where I parked". Psychologist Maria Alvarez describes forgetting in such cases as a filtering mechanism rather than a simple erasure. By weakening details that no longer guide useful action, the mind reduces competition among possible responses. This may partly explain why repeated routines become easier when irrelevant variations are ignored.

Experimental work on learning supports this interpretation. In a study of word-pair recall, participants first learned several associations and were later asked to practise only some of them. When tested afterwards, they remembered the practised pairs better but often recalled the unpractised pairs less accurately than a control group. This pattern, known as retrieval-induced forgetting, appears counterintuitive because practice is usually expected to strengthen memory in general. However, one interpretation holds that practising a target item suppresses related but currently unhelpful alternatives. The loss is therefore not accidental; it reflects a system prioritising access to information that has recently proved relevant.

Forgetting may also support abstraction. If a child remembers every encounter with every dog in exact sensory detail, forming the general concept "dog" could become unnecessarily difficult. Cognitive scientist Daniel Mercer argues that categories depend on the reduction of incidental features: the colour of a leash, the weather on a walk, or the sound of a particular owner's voice. What remains is a more stable pattern that can be applied to new cases. From this perspective, memory is useful not because it preserves experience perfectly, but because it edits experience into forms that can guide future judgement.

This argument has implications beyond childhood learning. Adults also rely on abstraction when they recognise a colleague in different clothes, understand a familiar word spoken in a new accent, or apply a rule learned in one setting to another. In each case, exact details from previous encounters are less important than the pattern that remains after variation has been reduced. Excessive attachment to surface detail could slow recognition rather than improve it.

The adaptive value of forgetting is especially clear in changing environments. Information that was once accurate may become misleading after circumstances shift. A commuter who remembers an old bus timetable too strongly may repeatedly arrive late after the schedule changes. Likewise, someone who relies on outdated instructions at work may make errors despite having an excellent memory for past procedures. Forgetting can therefore protect behaviour from being controlled by obsolete information. This does not mean that all loss is beneficial; rather, it suggests that a certain amount of weakening is necessary when the world no longer matches earlier experience.

There are, however, risks in celebrating forgetting too easily. Some forms of memory loss are distressing, socially costly, or medically serious. A student who forgets basic vocabulary cannot simply claim to be optimising cognition, and age-related memory disorders cannot be explained as useful adaptation. Critics such as neurologist Helen Sato warn that the language of efficiency may obscure genuine impairment. Her objection is important: any theory that presents forgetting as productive must distinguish between selective loss that improves performance and broad deterioration that undermines independence.

Nor is the boundary between useful and harmful forgetting always obvious. A professional who stops relying on outdated procedures may be adapting well, while a professional who forgets safety rules may be placing others at risk. Emotional memory raises a similar difficulty: reducing the vividness of painful events can help recovery, but losing the lessons attached to those events may create vulnerability. These examples show why the value of forgetting depends on context, timing, and the kind of information involved.

The most defensible conclusion is therefore a qualified one. Human memory is neither an archive designed for total preservation nor a careless storehouse from which items randomly vanish. It is a dynamic system shaped by attention, repetition, emotional significance, and present goals. Forgetting helps this system remain flexible, but only when it operates with sufficient selectivity. We should not admire every lapse, nor should we assume that perfect recall would be ideal. A useful memory is one that keeps enough of the past to guide action while allowing irrelevant or outdated details to recede.

This view also changes how learning should be evaluated. The aim is not simply to prevent all loss, but to strengthen information that remains meaningful while allowing minor, redundant, or misleading details to weaken. Effective study, professional training, and personal reflection all depend on this balance. Memory serves the future as much as the past, and forgetting is one way it avoids being trapped by experience that no longer deserves priority.
