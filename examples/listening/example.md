### Script: Part 2

Good morning and welcome to Westbury Community Library. I'll show you around, so please stay together.

The building you're standing in was originally constructed in 1874 as the home of a wealthy merchant called Sir Arthur Benson. It served as a family residence until 1923, when his descendants donated it to the town. In 1925 it opened as a public library, and we've been here ever since. We now hold around 35,000 volumes, making us one of the largest community libraries in the region.

We've just completed a major renovation of the children's section on the ground floor, with new furniture, interactive displays, and a storytelling corner. The reference room upstairs was refurbished five years ago, and the computer area was updated two years before that.

Before I show you around, let me go over a few practical points. All standard books can be borrowed for up to three weeks. If you need longer, you can renew online as long as nobody else has reserved the item. Overdue items that are returned within a week of the due date incur a small fine of 20 pence per day. If you're more than a week late, the system automatically applies a higher fine of 50 pence per day, and we'll send you an email reminder.

Now, let me explain where everything is located. As you come in through the main entrance, you're on the ground floor. Straight ahead is the information desk, and beyond that, to your left — that's the west wing — you'll find our local history archive. That's a popular resource for anyone researching family history. To the right, in the east wing, is the children's section. Our youngest visitors absolutely love the picture book area, which is right at the back of the east wing.

If you go up the main staircase to the first floor, on the north side you'll find our study carrels — individual desks with lamps where you can work quietly. The south side of the first floor houses our multimedia collection, including DVDs, audiobooks, and language learning resources.

We also have a meeting room available for community groups. That's actually on the mezzanine level, between the ground and first floors. It seats up to 30 people and can be booked free of charge by local residents. Finally, the basement level houses our archive storage and is not open to the public.

### Script: Part 4

Good morning everyone. Today's lecture is about the psychology of decision-making — a field that has transformed how we understand human behaviour in economics, public policy, and everyday life over the past several decades.

For a long time, traditional economic models assumed that human beings make rational decisions — that we weigh up all available information and choose the option that maximises our benefit. This idea, known as 'homo economicus', formed the foundation of classical economics for much of the twentieth century. But empirical research over the past five decades has revealed a far more complex and interesting picture. We are, it turns out, predictably irrational. Our decisions are shaped by mental shortcuts, emotional responses, and subtle features of the environment that traditional models simply did not account for.

What is particularly noteworthy is the dual-system framework developed by Daniel Kahneman, a psychologist who later won the Nobel Prize in Economics for his work. Kahneman proposed that human thinking operates through two distinct systems. System 1 is fast, automatic, and relies on intuition — it's the part of your brain that recognises a friend's face or brakes when a car stops suddenly. It operates with little or no effort and no sense of voluntary control. System 2, by contrast, is slow, deliberate, and analytical — it's what you use when solving a complex maths problem or deciding which insurance policy to choose. It requires conscious attention and can be easily disrupted. Most of the time, we operate on System 1, which is efficient for routine situations but prone to systematic errors in unfamiliar contexts.

Now, the identification of these errors — or cognitive biases — owes a great deal to the work of Amos Tversky, a close collaborator of Kahneman. Tversky conducted a series of elegant experiments that revealed how our thinking systematically departs from logic. He was instrumental in demonstrating the principle of loss aversion — the finding that losses typically feel about twice as powerful psychologically as equivalent gains. In practical terms, this means the pain of losing £50 is roughly twice as intense as the pleasure of gaining £50. This asymmetry has profound implications for everything from investment behaviour to marketing and consumer choice.

This brings us to the concept of the anchoring effect, which was also identified through the collaborative work of Tversky and Kahneman. In one famous experiment, participants were asked to estimate the percentage of African nations in the United Nations. Before giving their estimate, they were told a random number — either 10 or 65 — and asked whether the true figure was higher or lower. Those who started with 10 gave significantly lower estimates than those who started with 65. The initial number served as an anchor, pulling subsequent judgements toward it, even though the anchor was completely arbitrary and unrelated to the actual question. This effect has been replicated in countless contexts, from real estate pricing to salary negotiations.

Turning now to the practical applications of this research. Richard Thaler, a behavioural economist at the University of Chicago, extended these insights into the realm of public policy through what he called 'nudge' theory. Thaler argued that small changes in the way choices are presented — what he termed the choice architecture — can dramatically influence outcomes without restricting freedom or imposing mandates. For example, placing healthier foods at eye level in a cafeteria increases their selection without banning any options. Similarly, automatically enrolling employees into pension schemes rather than requiring them to opt in has been shown to dramatically increase retirement savings rates in several countries.

Another important concept is that of default options — the pre-set choice that takes effect if a person does nothing. One of the most striking demonstrations of this comes from research on organ donation. Cass Sunstein, a legal scholar who collaborated extensively with Thaler, examined how default options affect donation rates across different countries. In countries where citizens are automatically registered as organ donors unless they actively opt out — known as 'presumed consent' — donation rates exceed 90 per cent. In countries where citizens must actively opt in, rates often fall below 20 per cent. Sunstein's work showed convincingly that the default itself — not just people's underlying attitudes — was driving this enormous difference. This finding has led several governments, including Wales and Argentina, to reconsider their organ donation policies and shift toward presumed consent systems.

To summarise, the study of decision-making has moved from abstract models of rational choice to a rich, empirically-grounded understanding of how context, bias, and presentation shape our everyday judgements. The work of Kahneman, Tversky, Thaler, and Sunstein has not only deepened our theoretical understanding but has produced practical tools that policymakers and organisations now use to improve outcomes in health, finance, and beyond. In next week's lecture, we will examine how these principles apply specifically to consumer behaviour and financial decision-making in digital environments.
